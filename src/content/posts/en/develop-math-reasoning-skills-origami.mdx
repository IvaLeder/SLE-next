Did you know that origami is a great way to develop your math reasoning skills, and that teachers use it to teach geometry? Practicing origami also **works many skills at once**, from **hand-eye coordination** to **logical reasoning** and **problem-solving**.

So why not let your child start right now with this simple origami fortune teller and we promise, they will have a lot of fun doing it.

## What is an origami fortune teller and how to use it?

An origami fortune teller is a popular origami shape that can be used in children's games. Well, it’s not that popular anymore but every parent will probably still remember playing with this awesome toy. So why not revive good old game and introduce it to the new generation of kids?

But back to the fortune teller. In most origami fortune tellers, top four parts are labeled with colors and inside, we mark it usually with numbers. But any other shapes can serve as an alternative option. It is all about creativity. On the inside are eight flaps, and every flap contains a message and big fortune reveal.

The person who is predicting fortune is holding the fortune teller and follows instructions from the person whose fortune is being told. These messages may contain some descriptive attribute, event, animal or ask a person to answer the questions. They can also be some activities that the player must perform (great way to distribute those house chores). As with the markings on the flaps, it’s all about creativity and imagination.

Manipulation of origami fortune teller is done with index fingers and thumbs on both hands. The player chooses a color (or a number) and holder uses their fingers to switch between the two groups of colors and numbers inside the fortune teller. The holder switches the positions a number of times,  which may be determined by the number of letters in the color selected, the number originally chosen, or the sum of both. You can repeat this process a couple of times. After that, the player chooses one of the flaps which is then revealed. These flaps often have colors or numbers on them. The holder then lifts the flap and reveals the fortune underneath.

<Callout type="tip" title="Turn it into a learning game">
The flaps don't have to hide silly fortunes. Write **times-table questions**, spelling words, or "do 5 star jumps" under them and the fortune teller becomes a self-made quiz your child begs to play. Swapping the messages is a two-minute job, so you can match it to whatever they're learning this week.
</Callout>

## The math hiding in the folds

The title of this article promises math reasoning, so let's be specific about where it actually is - because none of it is obvious unless somebody points at it.

- **Every fold halves the paper.** Fold once and each side is 1/2. Fold again and you have 1/4, then 1/8. Your child is handling [fractions](/en/tools/fraction-visualizer) with their fingers before they ever meet them on a worksheet. Ask "how many of these little squares fit in the whole sheet?" and let them unfold it to check.
- **The diagonals are lines of symmetry.** Fold the square corner to corner and the two halves land exactly on top of each other. That's what symmetry *means*, and the creases draw it for you.
- **Flat becomes 3D.** A square sheet with no thickness turns into a thing with pockets that opens and closes. Getting from a 2D shape to a 3D object is real spatial reasoning, and it's the same skill an engineer uses reading a plan.
- **The game itself is counting.** "B-L-U-E" is four moves; picking 7 means seven more. Counting out loud while operating the fortune teller is arithmetic your child will happily do a hundred times.

You don't need to turn this into a lesson. Just ask the questions while you fold, and the math does its own work.

## Materials Needed to Make an Origami Fortune Teller

- Paper (squared, but plain A4 will also do the trick)
- Pencil
- 4 Coloring pencils (any color)

## What will you learn?

- Develop hand-eye coordination
- Attention and concentration
- Patience
- Math concepts, geometry, proportions and [fractions](/en/tools/fraction-visualizer) (every fold splits the paper into halves and quarters)
- Confidence and resistance to frustration
- Logical reasoning and problem solving

<KeyTakeaways>
- An **origami fortune teller** (or cootie catcher) is a folded-paper toy with colours or numbers outside and hidden messages under eight inner flaps.
- It's made from a single **square sheet** and needs no glue or scissors - just folding.
- The folding quietly teaches **fractions** (each fold halves the paper: 1/2, 1/4, 1/8), **symmetry** (the diagonal creases), and **2D-to-3D spatial reasoning**.
- Playing it is **counting practice**: spelling a colour and counting to a number are arithmetic children happily repeat.
- Swap the hidden messages for **times tables, spellings, or activities** to turn it into a custom learning game.
</KeyTakeaways>

## Frequently Asked Questions

<FAQ>
<Question question="How do you make an origami fortune teller?">
Start with a square sheet, fold all four corners into the centre, flip it over, and fold the corners into the centre again. Then fold it in half both ways to loosen it, slip your thumbs and index fingers under the four flaps, and it pops open. Watch the video or diagram above to follow along.
</Question>
<Question question="How do you play with a fortune teller?">
The holder works it with thumbs and index fingers. A player picks an outside colour, and you open and close the fortune teller once for each letter. Then they pick a number and you count that out too. Finally they choose a flap, and you lift it to reveal the hidden message.
</Question>
<Question question="What math does origami teach?">
Origami builds geometry and spatial reasoning. Each fold halves the paper, showing fractions; the diagonal creases demonstrate symmetry; and turning a flat square into a 3D object is the same spatial thinking an engineer uses to read a plan.
</Question>
<Question question="What paper do you need for a fortune teller?">
You need a square sheet. Origami paper is ideal, but you can make a square from ordinary A4 by folding one corner across and trimming off the extra strip. Add a pencil and a few coloured pencils to label the flaps.
</Question>
<Question question="What age is origami good for?">
Simple models like the fortune teller suit children from around 6, once they can make reasonably accurate folds. Younger children can join in with help, and the activity scales up easily - older kids can add math questions or trickier designs.
</Question>
<Question question="Why is origami good for children's development?">
Origami works several skills at once: fine motor control and hand-eye coordination from precise folding, patience and focus from following steps, and logical and spatial reasoning from turning a plan into a finished shape - all while feeling like play.
</Question>
</FAQ>
