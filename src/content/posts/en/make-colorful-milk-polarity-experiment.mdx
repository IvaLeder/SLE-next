## What is Polarity?

**Chemical Polarity** is a property of a molecule that describes the distribution of the electrical charge inside of it. When atoms inside of molecule don’t have the same electronegativity, atoms inside of molecule are not distributed equally. And that causes an asymmetrical or polar electric field.

You probably noticed that some liquids mix well and some don’t. For example, water and sugar mix while water and oil keep separated. Why is that? Molecules of the different polarities don’t mix, in this case, both sugar and water are polar molecules and the oil is nonpolar.

<Figure src="/images/posts/Polar-and-nonpolar-molecules-difference.jpg" alt="Polar and nonpolar molecules difference" width="600" height="428">
  Polar molecules are attracted to other polar molecules while they are repelled by nonpolar molecules.
</Figure>

The molecule is **polar** when there is a difference in **electronegativity** of its atoms. And molecule is **nonpolar** when there is similar electronegativity between its atoms. Electronegativity describes how easy is for an atom to receive electrons.

If you want to know more details about polarity, please read our [**Lava lamp experiment**](/en/make-lava-lamp/#what-is-polarity) article, where we discuss the topic of polarity even further.

## The Science Behind the Colorful Milk Experiment

Milk contains a few different ingredients, mostly **water**, but also some **minerals, proteins, and fat**. And we already learned water is a polar molecule while all fats are nonpolar.

But what about the **dish soap**? Soap is used primarily for washing different types of **grease** and dirt, stuff that water can’t dissolve by itself. And to be able to do that effectively it must be able to mix with nonpolar molecules. 

On the other hand, we know that we can mix soap with water and get a bubbly solution. How is that? Well, a soap molecule is made out of a **long nonpolar tail and short polar head**, so it can mix with both types of molecules!

<Figure src="/images/posts/How-to-Make-Colorful-Milk-Polarity-Experiment-Dishwashing-soap-molecule.jpg" alt="How to Make Colorful Milk Polarity Experiment - Dishwashing soap molecule" width="600" height="428">
  Soaps have both polar and nonpolar molecules and that is why they are so great at cleaning.
</Figure>

When we put soap into the milk, the nonpolar tail will collect fat molecules, while the head will mingle with the water molecules. In all that movement, the food colors are pushed around and we can observe a real visual spectacle.

## Materials Needed for the Colorful Milk Experiment

<Figure src="/images/posts/How-to-Make-Colorful-Milk-Polarity-Experiment-Materials-needed.jpg" alt="How to Make Colorful Milk Polarity Experiment - Materials needed" width="600" height="428">
  We will need some Milk, Food colors, and Dishwashing soap for our polarity experiment.
</Figure>

1.  **Plate.** Any kitchen plate you find will be good here as long as it is able to hold liquid.
2.  **Milk.** 1 deciliter of milk will be enough. Make sure you cover the whole bottom of the plate with milk. That will be enough for our experiment. Also, milk with more fat will be even better for this experiment.
3.  **Food colors.** Food colors are light enough to easily move on the surface of milk. We can also try with different colors - like acrylic colors for example. It’s advisable to use 2 or more colors for the best visual effect.
4.  **Dishwashing soap.** Any dishwashing soap you have in the kitchen will be good. Only 1 drop is needed for our polarity experiment.
5.  **Cotton swab.** A cotton swab would be useful since, with it, we can easily apply a small amount of dishwashing soap to our experiment. If you don’t have a cotton swab, you can just pour 1 drop of dishwashing soap on the plate.

## Instructions for the Colorful Milk Experiment

If you would rather watch the video on how to do the activity, you can find it at the beginning of the article. And for step by step instructions, continue reading below.

1.  **Pour the milk into the plate**. Depending on the plate size, you will need enough milk to cover the bottom of the plate.
2.  **Put a few drops of food coloring in the middle of the plate**. Be sure to add a few drops of each color you decide to use. Food colors are great here. If you decide to use acrylic colors or some other colors that are thick or dense, you can use a few drops of water to dilute them.
3.  **Soak the cotton swab into the dishwashing soap**. Sink one side of the cotton swab into the bottle of dishwashing soap to soak it. That will be enough for the activity. If you don’t have a cotton swab, you can just add 1 or 2 drops of dishwashing soap into the middle of the plate.
4.  **Touch the milk with a cotton swab**. Touch the middle of the plate, where you added colors with a cotton swab soaked with dishwashing soap. Or add one or two drops of soap to the middle of the plate. Enjoy the spectacle of the dancing and moving colors!

<Callout type="tip">
Use whole (full-fat) milk for the biggest, longest-lasting color explosion  - the more fat there is, the more the soap has to grab onto. Skim milk barely moves. When the colors slow down, dip a fresh swab in soap and touch a new spot to send them dancing again.
</Callout>

## What will you develop and learn

- **Chemistry knowledge**. By conducting this experiment, we will learn about polarity and molecules. This will develop our knowledge about chemistry and chemical processes.
- **Concept of polarity**. We will learn about polar and nonpolar molecules. Also, we will learn about electronegativity and how molecules interact with each other.
- **What is dishwashing soap and how does it work**. Dishwashing soap has a unique molecular structure and we will learn all about it.
- **Mixing colors.** We will improve and/or refresh our knowledge about colors and how they mix and how can mixing create different colors.
- **How to conduct an experiment.** We will conduct experiment and learn about basic concepts of the experimental method.

<KeyTakeaways>
- **Polar** molecules (like water) mix with other polar molecules; **nonpolar** ones (like fat) keep separate.
- A **soap molecule has a polar head and a nonpolar tail**, so it can grab both water and fat at once.
- Adding soap makes it rush to grab the **fat** in the milk, and that motion pushes the food coloring around  - the "dance."
- **Full-fat milk** gives the best show; skim milk barely moves.
- It's the same trick soap uses to wash **grease** off your dishes.
</KeyTakeaways>

## Frequently Asked Questions

<FAQ>
<Question question="Why does the milk move when you add dish soap?">
Soap molecules have a nonpolar tail that's attracted to the fat in the milk. When you add soap, the molecules dart around trying to grab the fat, and all that motion pushes the drops of food coloring with them  - which looks like the colors are dancing.
</Question>
<Question question="Why does whole milk work better than skim milk?">
The reaction is driven by the fat in the milk, so more fat means more movement. Whole milk has plenty of fat for the soap to chase, giving a big, long-lasting swirl. Skim milk has almost no fat, so the colors barely move.
</Question>
<Question question="Does the milk experiment work with water instead of milk?">
Not really  - plain water has no fat for the soap to react with, so you won't get the dramatic swirling. The fat is the key ingredient, which is why milk (especially full-fat) works so well.
</Question>
<Question question="Why do the colors eventually stop moving?">
The colors move until the soap has spread out and coated all the fat it can reach and everything reaches balance. To restart the show, dip a fresh swab in soap and touch a new spot on the plate.
</Question>
<Question question="Is the colorful milk experiment safe for kids?">
Yes. It uses ordinary milk, food coloring, and a drop of dish soap, all safe to handle. Just don't drink the soapy milk afterward, and wash hands if food coloring gets on them (it can stain briefly).
</Question>
<Question question="What kind of soap works best?">
Regular liquid dishwashing soap works best because it's designed to break down grease and fat. One drop is enough. Hand soap or body wash will work too, but dish soap gives the strongest effect.
</Question>
</FAQ>
