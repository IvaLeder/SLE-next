## What is the air pressure?

Atmospheric or **air pressure** is a **force** applied on a surface by the air above because of the influence of **gravity**. Gravity is pulling down the air molecules and they are pressing their weight on top of the Earth’s surface.

<Figure src="/images/posts/Air-Pressure-Diagram.jpg" alt="Diagram showing atmospheric air pressure pressing equally on all sides of a can" width="600" height="428">
  In humid air, the pressure is lower than when the air is dry.
</Figure>

The pressure is highest **at sea level**, where there is the highest density of those air molecules. As the altitude increases, the atmospheric pressure drops.

The amount of oxygen in the air also decreases with the pressure decreasing. The higher we go, the lower the pressure and amount of oxygen. That is why at high altitudes, we can feel dizzy and sick due to the lack of oxygen.

Besides altitude, the amount of **humidity** is an important influence on air pressure. Water molecules in the air, contained in the water vapor, weigh less than the oxygen (and other gas molecules) so as the air becomes more humid, it’s lighter and therefore the pressure is lower.

At sea level, air pressure affecting our body is approximately 15 tons! But that pressure won’t crush us since there is also an inner pressure of equal size so they nullify each other.

The atmospheric pressure is measured using a **barometer**. As the weight of the atmosphere changes, mercury in a glass tube rises or falls. The mercury rises when the atmospheric pressure is high.

## Materials Needed for the Air Pressure Experiment

<Figure src="/images/posts/Materials-Needed-for-Can-Crush-Experiment.jpg" alt="Can crush experiment materials: an empty soda can, a bowl of water, kitchen tongs, and a stove" width="600" height="428">
  All we need is an empty Soda Can, Bowl with Water, Kitchen Tongs, and a Heater.
</Figure>

- **Empty can.** A standard soda can or beer can will do great for our experiment. These aluminum cans can be easily obtained in any grocery store since many beverages come packed in cans.
- **Bowl of Water**. Put some cold water into a bowl or a basin. It doesn’t have to be ice cold, just regular cold water from the pipe will be enough.
- **Kitchen Tongs**. We will need something to hold a hot can, and kitchen tongs are the best tool we can use for that.
- **Heater** or **stove.** For getting the water inside of the can boiling, we need a heater. We can use our kitchen stove to achieve our goal.
- Optional: **Spoon** and **Funnel**. The spoon and funnel will help us in adding a little bit of water inside of the can. We can just add water directly from the pipe so this is optional. But for demonstration purposes, it is good to show adding water in the can.

## Instructions on doing the Can Crush Experiment

If you’re interested in a video guide, you can watch the video at the beginning of the article. And for step-by-step instructions and explanations, continue reading further.

<Callout type="safety">
This is a hot experiment. The can and the steam get scalding, so an adult should handle the heating and always use kitchen tongs to move the can. Flip and dunk it carefully - the can stays hot right up until the moment it hits the water.
</Callout>

1.  **Put one or two tablespoons of water** (around 15 milliliters) **into the empty can**. Here you can use a funnel and spoon to demonstrate the procedure. Or you can add the same amount of water directly from the pipe if you are not demonstrating the experiment to other people.
2.  **Prepare cold water in some container** that is big enough to sink the can in it. We will do that in just a minute.
3.  **Heat the can until the water inside starts to boil**. For this, you can use the stove or a heater. Since the amount of water inside the can is small to hear water bubbling, you can tell that it is boiling when you notice steam coming out of the can.
4.  **Use the kitchen tongs** to grab the can. Be careful, the can is very hot so use tongs to not get burned. Turn the can upside down and **sink it in the cold water container**.
5.  You will instantly **hear a loud pop** and the can will crush because of the pressure difference.

## Science Behind The Air Pressure Experiment

So what happened to the can? Why did it get crushed? By heating up our can, we boiled the water inside it. The process of boiling turned the water into **vapor.** And since the water vapor molecules are much more spread out than the water molecules, they take more space and are forcing the molecules of air out from the can.

And when we put the can in the cold water, we suddenly cooled it. That cooling caused the water vapor in the can to condense, creating a **partial vacuum**. Because of that, the pressure outside of the can became much greater than the pressure inside, and that **pressure difference crushed the can**.

<Figure src="/images/posts/Reason-of-Can-Crush-and-Implosion.jpg" alt="Diagram of why the can implodes: steam condenses and outside air pressure crushes it" width="600" height="428">
  As the air leaves the can, it gets crushed by the air pressure from outside. That is called an implosion.
</Figure>

So in summary, a **can will get crushed when the pressure outside is greater than the pressure inside** of the can. Also, the pressure **difference must be greater than the one the can is able to withstand**. For example, we can easily crush an aluminum can with our hands. When we squeeze the can, the pressure outside becomes greater than the pressure inside. If we squeeze hard enough the can collapses.

This phenomenon also has a name - **implosion!** As you may guess, an implosion is the opposite of an explosion. Implosion is a process in which objects are destroyed by collapsing on themselves. Implosion **reduces the volume** of an object and concentrates matter and energy into a smaller space.

We may also ask ourselves: "Won't the water from the bowl fill the can through the hole when we sink the can?" Some water may actually come inside of the can, but the water cannot flow into the can fast enough to fill it before the air outside crushes it.

<Callout type="tip" title="🔬 Make it a real experiment">
Try changing the water you dunk the can into. Crush one can in ice-cold water and another in lukewarm water, keeping the heating exactly the same, and see which crumples more completely. The colder the dunk, the faster the steam turns back to liquid, so the colder can should crush harder. An adult handles the hot can with tongs.
</Callout>

<KeyTakeaways>
- **Air pressure** is the weight of the atmosphere pressing on everything, about 15 tons on your body, balanced by equal pressure inside you.
- Boiling the water in the can turns it to **steam**, which pushes most of the air out.
- Plunging the can into cold water makes the steam **condense**, leaving a partial vacuum inside.
- The outside air pressure is now far greater than the inside, so it crushes the can: an **implosion**.
- An implosion is the opposite of an explosion: the object collapses inward on itself.
</KeyTakeaways>

## Frequently Asked Questions

<FAQ>
<Question question="Why does the can get crushed?">
Heating boils the water in the can into steam, which drives the air out. When you plunge the hot can into cold water, the steam instantly condenses back to liquid, leaving a partial vacuum inside. The much greater air pressure outside then crushes the can.
</Question>
<Question question="What is an implosion?">
An implosion is the opposite of an explosion. Instead of bursting outward, an object collapses inward on itself, because the pressure outside is far greater than the pressure inside. The can crush is a classic small-scale implosion.
</Question>
<Question question="Why do you put water in the can before heating it?">
That little bit of water is what makes the trick work. As it boils it turns to steam that fills the can and pushes the air out. Without it there would be nothing to condense later, and no sudden drop in pressure to crush the can.
</Question>
<Question question="Why does the can have to be flipped upside down into the water?">
Turning it over puts the opening straight into the water, which instantly seals it and cools the steam at the same time. The steam condenses in a flash, so the vacuum forms before outside air can sneak in through the opening.
</Question>
<Question question="Doesn't water just fill the can through the opening?">
A little water does get in, but it can't flow through the small opening fast enough to fill the can before the air pressure has already crushed it. The crushing happens almost instantly.
</Question>
<Question question="Is the can crush experiment safe for kids?">
It involves a stove and a scalding-hot can, so an adult should do the heating and handle the can with kitchen tongs. With that supervision it's a safe and dramatic demonstration for children to watch.
</Question>
</FAQ>
