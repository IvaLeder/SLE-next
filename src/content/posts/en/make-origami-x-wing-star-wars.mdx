## What is X-Wing?

X-Wing is a type of starfighter used by the Rebel Alliance and is famous for its role in the destruction of the Death Star. The name comes from their distinctive “wings” that would spread in attack formation, making an X shape.

X-Wing is a one-man starfighter that also has a slot for a [droid](https://wiki.kidzsearch.com/wiki/Droid). It has four laser cannons and two proton torpedo launchers. It also has energy shields for defense.  Its four wings (s-foils) can fold open or closed. It has four engines and can use hyperspace travel.

## Materials Needed for Origami X-Wing

- **Paper**. One plain A4 piece of paper will be enough to make our origami X-wing. But we recommend getting some colored paper for a more lively X-wing. A specialized origami paper would be the best, but as mentioned, it is not required.
- Bonus: **Colored Pencils**. We can use Colored Pencils to decorate our origami X-wing however we want and make it truly unique.

<Callout type="tip" title="Sharp creases make sharp ships">
The X-wing has more folds than a boat or a pig, so precision matters. Press each fold flat with your fingernail (or the edge of a ruler) to get a crisp crease, and take the folds slowly - a neat, symmetrical model depends on the early folds lining up exactly.
</Callout>

## What Will You Develop And Learn By Making Origami X-Wing Fighter?

- **Motor skills** and **hand-eye coordination**. Creating origami requires a precise folding paper technique. That is why we will develop the fine motor skills and hand-eye coordination that are required for precise movements. So making any origami shape is an excellent way to develop and improve our precise hand and finger movements.
- **Attention** and **concentration.** Origami, the art of paper folding, requires going through multiple steps, analyzing how to fold next, and sometimes going back to correct the step that may be wrong. All that requires focus, attention, and concentration. And origami will develop these skills while we are focusing on completing our model.
- **Creativity**. Although it may seem that origami follows predetermined, specific steps, there is also room for creativity and finding folding methods that work better for us. Also, freeform folding will use our creative muscles, and maybe we even create new origami shapes? Someone had to come up with all the amazing origami shapes that we create, right?
- **Confidence**, **patience,** and **resistance to frustration.** Finishing origami models, especially complex ones like X-wing creates an amazing sense of accomplishment. It takes a lot of patience and sometimes starting from scratch to finish some shapes. All that improves our patience and teaches us how to cope with frustration when we get stuck.
- **Procedural memory**. We all start with diagrams, videos, or other instructions when making new origami models. But after making a couple of them, we will learn them and won’t need any help to make them. This is how our procedural memory gets developed - we learn all steps by doing origami.

## Frequently Asked Questions

<FAQ>
<Question question="How do you make an origami X-wing?">
Fold it from a single square or A4 sheet, following the diagram or video above. The model builds up the body and the four wings through a series of folds, so work slowly and keep each crease sharp. It's trickier than a boat or pig, so it suits older or more experienced folders.
</Question>
<Question question="Why is it called an X-wing?">
The name comes from the ship's four wings, called s-foils, which spread apart into an X shape when the fighter goes into attack formation. Closed, they sit together; opened, they form the distinctive X the ship is famous for.
</Question>
<Question question="Is the origami X-wing hard to make?">
It's moderate - harder than a boat, house, or pig because it has more folds and relies on symmetry. If you're new to origami, start with a simpler model first, then come back to the X-wing once your folds are neat and accurate.
</Question>
<Question question="What paper do you need for an origami X-wing?">
One sheet of plain A4 is enough, but coloured or proper origami paper gives a livelier result and often holds creases better. Grey paper makes it look most like the real starfighter, and coloured pencils let you add markings.
</Question>
<Question question="What does making origami teach you?">
Origami develops fine motor skills and hand-eye coordination, focus and patience, spatial and geometric reasoning, and procedural memory - once you've made a model a few times, you can fold it without instructions. It also leaves room for creativity and problem solving.
</Question>

The X-wing is a one-pilot Rebel Alliance starfighter, famous for destroying the Death Star. It carries four laser cannons and two proton torpedo launchers, has energy shields and four engines, and can travel through hyperspace, with a slot for an astromech droid.
</Question>
</FAQ>
