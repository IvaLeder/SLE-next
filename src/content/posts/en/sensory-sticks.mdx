<Callout type="safety" title="Safety first">
A baby will explore the sticks with their mouth first, so nothing can be a choking hazard. Glue every piece down really well and give each one a firm tug before every play session - any button or pompom that comes loose goes straight into the mouth, so anything that could detach must be too big to swallow. The glue gun burns, so keep it away from children and do the gluing yourself. Always supervise children under 3.
</Callout>

## Sensorimotor Development and Why It Matters

We talked a lot about sensorimotor development and its importance in [Sensorimotor development and activities for Children](/en/sensorimotor-activities-children). If you are interested in a more theoretical overview, we recommend reading it.

Let’s just briefly go over the most important concepts.

Children explore the world through their senses. Everything is new and exciting to them - they must **touch**, **lick** and **chew** to understand and construct their environment. Freedom to explore nurtures their curiosity and allows them to **improve their cognitive and motor skills**.

That’s one of the basic needs of a small child and we as parents must support it. The key is to construct an environment that is interesting and stimulating but also safe. There should be many different stimuli - **textures**, **sounds**, **contrasts** and child should engage with them actively - as it interests him/her, not in some way prescribed by us.

This early sensory exploration is the basis for all other learning. If a child is curious and engaged, it will try to move towards the object of interest, grasp it, throw it. So **motoric development is closely intertwined with this sensory exploration**.

## Tips for Making and Using Sensory Sticks

What I love about the sensory sticks is that there is no “right” way to make them. You can really get creative with the materials - **old socks**, **carpets**, **sponges**, **buttons** - **possibilities are endless**. Use many different textures - **fluffy**, **hard**, **soft**, **coarse** - the more the merrier.

Different materials promote curiosity and allow the child to experience a full range of stimuli. Just keep the safety note above in mind when you choose them - a baby will explore each stick with their mouth first, so everything must be firmly glued and too big to swallow.

This is a great toy to have on the go - small, won’t take much space but can provide tons of entertainment. Best thing is, they are so easy to make, you can make the new ones every few days to keep them fresh!

## Frequently Asked Questions

<FAQ>
<Question question="What are sensory sticks?">
Sensory sticks are wooden craft (popsicle) sticks decorated with a mix of textures - felt, buttons, sponge, fabric, fluffy balls - glued along each one. A child explores the different surfaces by touch (and mouth), which stimulates the senses and builds fine motor skills.
</Question>
<Question question="What age are sensory sticks good for?">
They suit babies from around 6 months, who explore the textures by touch and mouth, through toddlers, who grip and compare them. Because babies mouth everything, make sure every piece is firmly attached and too big to swallow, and always supervise.
</Question>
<Question question="How do you make sensory sticks?">
Choose a variety of textured materials, warm up a glue gun, and stick them firmly along wooden craft sticks - felt, buttons, sponge, and fluffy balls all work well. Clean off any excess glue, tug-test each piece, and they're ready for play.
</Question>
<Question question="Why do babies put sensory sticks in their mouth?">
A baby's mouth is their most sensitive exploring tool, rich with nerve endings long before their fingers become precise. "Mouthing" is how they investigate texture, shape, and size - it's normal learning, which is why sensory sticks must be built to be completely choke-safe.
</Question>
<Question question="Are sensory sticks safe for babies?">
Yes, when made carefully. Glue every piece down well, tug-test them before each play session, and make sure anything that could come loose is too big to swallow. Keep the hot glue gun away from children, and supervise babies and toddlers throughout play.
</Question>
<Question question="Why are sensory sticks good for travelling?">
They're small, light, and take up almost no space, yet offer plenty of tactile entertainment - ideal for a bag or pocket on trips. They're also so quick and cheap to make that you can whip up fresh ones with new textures whenever the old set loses its novelty.
</Question>
</FAQ>
