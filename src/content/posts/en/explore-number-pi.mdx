## What is Pi?

Let’s start with a little history lesson. People were measuring circles from ancient times. And while measuring, they noticed peculiar rule. They measured the **diameter** \- line connecting two dots on the circle through the center. They measured **circumference** \- the length of the circle if it would be opened up and formed a straight line. **The ratio** of the circumference of a circle to its diameter was always around 3. No matter the size of the circle. Written sources from around 1900 BC show two different guesses at the value: the **Babylonians** used 25/8 (3.125), while the Egyptian **Rhind papyrus** works out to (16/9)², which is about 3.16. Both are impressively close for four thousand years ago.

**Archimedes**, the famous Greek mathematician, took it even further. Instead of measuring circles, he **calculated** - and that is the whole point of his method: he worked out the perimeters of two polygons, one inscribed and one circumscribed to a circle. **Polygon** is just a 2D shape formed from the straight lines.  The name of the polygon tells you how many sides the shape has. For example, a triangle has 3 sides, quadrilateral 4, pentagon 5, etc. **Inscribed** polygon just means that whole polygon is surrounded by a circle and **circumscribed** means opposite, the circle is inside the polygon. Starting with a hexagon, he calculated the perimeter of the inscribed and circumscribed polygon. He knew that the circumference of the circle has to be between those two values. Then he doubled the sides of the polygon to 12, 24, 48 and finally 96. By using a 96-sided polygon to calculate perimeters, he approximated that pi must be between 223/71 and 22/7 (3.1408 < π < 3.1429). Pretty close!

An invention of calculus brought new ways to calculate pi and the advent of computing made it simpler and faster. Nowadays, pi is calculated to over **300 trillion** (!!!) digits using computer programs and various clever algorithms. It’s mostly for bragging rights though. NASA is rounding pi to 15 digits for all their calculations. And to calculate the circumference of a visible universe (with a radius of 46 billion light-years) you would need only about 40 digits to get the accuracy equal to the diameter of the smallest atom (H). That’s amazing!

## Some interesting tidbits about pi

Pi can **never be expressed exactly** in the terms of a fraction, like one integer over the other. That means it is an **irrational number**. And indeed, it goes on forever, without repeating patterns of digits. 3.14159265358979323846… It’s already been calculated to hundreds of trillions of decimal places!

How do we know it is irrational and by that definition never ends? Well, that’s been proved in the 18th century by Johann Heinrich Lambert by using a combination of trigonometry and proof by contradiction. He also correctly suggested that pi is a **transcendental number**. That means that no polynomial equation with whole-number coefficients (example x² − 4x + 2 = 0) can have pi as its solution. That was finally proved by Ferdinand von Lindemann in 1882.

And although we know it as pi (Greek letter 𝛑), this name first appeared in the 18th century. It was also called **Archimedes’ constant**. Mathematicians believe that any pattern of numbers you can think of appears somewhere in the forever going digits of pi - although, amazingly, no one has managed to prove that yet. ## The significance of Pi in Modern Science

As you can imagine, pi is very important when dealing with round 2d and 3d shapes. It’s needed in any task that deals with **calculating volume and area** of spheres, cylinders, cones, and similar shapes. It is also used to **determine the rotations** of any circular objects.

That’s why it’s very important in astronomy - determining properties of planetary bodies. NASA is using pi in many of their exploration quests. For example, pi was used in modeling distributions for the rover’s landing location on Mars. It also helps to determine which newly discovered planets can support life. For that, they must be in the optimal position from the star, not too close and not too far. By using pi in their calculation, scientists can approximate the planet’s location and is it in the habitable area. Calculating distance between stars is also using this special kind of geometry, “**spherical geometry**” that heavily utilizes pi.

Besides use in geometry, pi has its uses in **number theory** as well as **statistics**. It is really all around us (pun intended!).

## Instructions on how to do demonstrate Pi

You can check a video on how to make a simple demonstration of number Pi at the beginning of the article. Or if you prefer reading, continue and see the instructions below.

1. Take a piece of cardboard and draw a circle by using the compass.
2. Cut out the circle with the scissors.
3. Draw a line from one side of the circle to the other through the center - that’s the diameter of the circle. Measure it and write down the result.
4. Take a piece of yarn and measure how much length of it you need to go all around the circle. That’s the circumference of a circle.
5. Repeat this process a few times with different sized circles.
6. Calculate the ratio of circumference to diameter (divide the circumference by the diameter). Is it close to 3? It should actually be 3.14159… It’s pi! Probably not perfectly accurate because of the imprecise way of measuring, but should be close.
7. Now turn that around and you have the formula. You just found that circumference ÷ diameter = π, which means **C = π × d**. The circumference is simply pi times the distance across. That's the formula your measuring tape just proved.
8. And the version from school, **C = 2πr**, is the very same formula. The radius r is half the diameter, so d = 2r - swap that in and C = π × d becomes C = 2πr. Two ways of writing one idea, and now neither is something to memorize blindly.

<Callout type="tip" title="Skip the cardboard, raid the kitchen">
Cutting a neat circle is fiddly, and wrapping yarn around a wobbly cardboard disc is fiddlier. Ready-made circles work better: a **tin can, a jar lid, a plate, a mug, a bicycle wheel**. The yarn sits snugly around a can, and measuring across a lid with a ruler is easy. The real payoff is doing several very different sizes - when the tiny jar lid and the big plate both come out at 3.14, your child can *see* that pi doesn't care how big the circle is. That's the whole lesson, and it lands harder from a plate than from a formula.
</Callout>

## Frequently Asked Questions

<FAQ>
<Question question="What is pi in simple terms?">
Pi (π) is the number you get when you divide any circle's circumference (the distance around it) by its diameter (the distance across it). It's always about 3.14, and it's the same for every circle no matter how big or small.
</Question>
<Question question="Why is pi always about 3.14?">
Because every circle has the same shape, scaled up or down. The circumference always works out to a little more than three times the diameter, so dividing one by the other always gives the same number - pi.
</Question>
<Question question="What is the formula for a circle's circumference?">
The circumference is C = 2 × π × r, where r is the radius (half the diameter). Since the diameter is 2r, this is the same as saying the circumference equals pi times the diameter.
</Question>
<Question question="Does pi ever end?">
No. Pi is an irrational number, which means its digits carry on forever without ever settling into a repeating pattern. It has been calculated to trillions of digits, but it can never be written out exactly.
</Question>
<Question question="Who discovered pi?">
People noticed the constant ratio thousands of years ago, the Babylonians and Egyptians estimated it around 1900 BC. The Greek mathematician Archimedes was the first to calculate it rigorously, using polygons, which is why pi is sometimes called Archimedes' constant.
</Question>
<Question question="Why is pi important in science?">
Pi appears in any calculation involving circles, spheres, cylinders, or rotation, from the area of a wheel to the orbit of a planet. NASA uses pi to plan spacecraft trajectories and even to help judge whether distant planets could support life.
</Question>
</FAQ>
