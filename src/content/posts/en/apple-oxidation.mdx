## What is Oxidation?

In simple terms, **oxidation is a** **loss of electrons in an element** or a compound **in contact with the oxygen** in the air. The opposite process is called reduction. **Reduction** is the process where a compound or an element **gains electrons** in the contact with oxygen.

<Figure src="/images/posts/apple-oxidation-redox-en.png" alt="Oxidation: a molecule loses an electron; Reduction: a molecule gains an electron" width="1500" height="1000">
  Oxidation is when an element loses electrons and Reduction when it gains electrons.
</Figure>

The most well-known example of oxidation is when iron combines with oxygen to form iron oxide or **rust**. In these situations, we say that iron has oxidized into rust.

After the discovery of electrons, scientists concluded that oxidation and reduction are happening at the same time. That process is called **Redox reaction** or **Oxidation-reduction.**

Some elements, such as Sodium, Magnesium, and Iron lose electrons more easily than the others - we say they are **easily oxidized**. On the other hand, there are elements that hold their electrons more tightly, like Nitrogen and Chlorine - they are **hard to oxidize**.

Interestingly, oxidation doesn’t necessarily involve oxygen! Oxygen was the first discovered oxidant so the name stayed until today. There is [another oxidation definition that includes hydrogen](https://www.thoughtco.com/definition-of-oxidation-in-chemistry-605456). Here, oxidation is the loss of hydrogen, while reduction is the gain of hydrogen. This is the opposite of the oxygen definition, so it may be confusing. That’s why it is good to be aware of this alternative definition of oxidation.

## What are the Antioxidants?

**Antioxidants** are the **molecules that prevent oxidation in other molecules**. Why is that important? Most life on the Earth requires oxygen for its survival. However, oxidation in our body can lead to the **production of free radicals** which may damage our cells.

<Figure src="/images/posts/apple-oxidation-antioxidants-en.png" alt="A free radical steals an electron from a healthy atom; an antioxidant donates an electron and makes it stable again" width="1500" height="1000">
  Antioxidants prevent the creation of free radicals and slow down oxidation.
</Figure>

If your body has high levels of free radicals, it can cause harm. Free radicals are linked to a variety of diseases, including diabetes, heart disease, and cancer. But they are mostly kept in check by our body's antioxidant defenses.

Antioxidants are slowing down that process of oxidation - in theory, they could slow down the aging process and prevent the development of certain diseases. The best-known antioxidants in the human body are vitamins C and E and can be found in food, especially plant-based foods like fruit and vegetable. Lemon is especially rich in vitamin C because of the lemon acid. Lemon acid (just like all other acids) has lower pH values and that is why they slow down reactions with oxygen.

But next to being good for our bodies, antioxidants are used as food preservatives too! In the food preservation process, antioxidants are compounds that **delay or prevent the deterioration** **of foods** by oxidative mechanisms. It involves the addition of an oxygen atom or the removal of a hydrogen atom from the different chemical molecules found in food.

## The science behind the Apple Oxidation Activity

Why is the apple quickly turning brown when peeled off or sliced? Apple contains a peel or shell that protects the inside of an apple. When the inner part of the apple is exposed to air, browning in apples occurs. That browning process is called **enzymatic browning** or **oxidation reaction**.

<Figure src="/images/posts/apple-oxidation-lemon-effect-en.png" alt="Apple cross-section: the half without lemon juice browns from oxygen, the half with lemon juice stays fresh" width="1500" height="1000">
  Citric acid from Lemon is a strong antioxidant and prevents oxidation on an apple slice.
</Figure>

That’s because the oxygen-reactive enzyme is found under the surface (skin) layer. These enzymes are called **phenols** and they are found in the cells of the apple. The brown color of the skin is caused by the **phenols being transformed into melanin**. The phenolase enzymes need to be protected in order to stop the reaction. And that can be achieved by using **heat** or **acid.**

**Citric acid** from the lemon serves as an **antioxidant** in our experiment. All acids slow down the oxidation process because of their low pH. Oxygen will first react with acid and only when all acid has been depleted will it react with the apple enzymes. Then we will see an apple turning brown.

## Materials needed for an Apple Oxidation Experiment with Lemon Acid

<Figure src="/images/posts/Materials-Needed-for-Apple-Oxidation-Experiment.jpg" alt="Materials Needed for Apple Oxidation Experiment" width="600" height="428">
  All you need for the experiment are simple kitchen materials: Apple, Lemon, Knife, Cup, and Brush.
</Figure>

- **Apple.** One apple will be enough. The fresher apple will make the experiment more clear but any apple will suffice. Just make sure you are able to make at least 2 apple slices.
- **Lemon.** One lemon or even one slice of lemon will be enough. We just need to apply citric acid to one slice of the apple, and for that, we just need a small amount of lemon acid.
- **Cup or bowl.** Cup will help us to squeeze lemon in it so we can apply lemon acid more easily to the apple. But it’s entirely optional.
- **Brush.** As with the cup, the brush can help us to apply lemon acid to the apple slice more precisely for better experiment effects. But we can even soak the apple in acid or apply in any other way so the brush is also optional.
- **Kitchen knife.** To slice the apple. It shouldn’t be a problem to get a kitchen knife so we can cut 2, same-sized apple slices for the best comparison of experiment results.

## Instruction for Apple Oxidation Experiment

For video instruction on how to do this experiment, watch the “how-to” video at the beginning of an article or continue reading for step-by-step instructions.

<Callout type="safety">
Slicing the apple calls for a kitchen knife, so an adult should handle the cutting. Once the slices are ready, kids can safely take over the fun part:brushing on the lemon juice and checking the apple every hour.
</Callout>

1.  **Cut an apple** in half, so you have 2 equal apple pieces.
2.  Take the **lemon** you have prepared and **squeeze it into a cup**. Or you can directly squeeze the lemon juice on one apple slice if you want to speed up the process.
3.  Take a brush, dip it into the **lemon juice**, and **spread it over one apple slice**. This will be our experiment slice where we try to prevent the oxidation process. Don’t apply anything to the other slice. We will see what happens to the apple if we don’t apply any food preservatives to it.

    <Figure src="/images/posts/Applying-lemon-juice-to-apple.jpg" alt="Applying lemon juice to apple" width="600" height="428">
      Apply lemon juice on one apple slice and leave the other as it is.
    </Figure>

4.  **Leave your 2 apple slices at room temperature** for a couple of hours. You can check your apple slices every hour to see if there is any difference. You should notice the differences between the 2 slices already after one hour.
5.  **Check the state of your apple halves** after 1, 2, and 3 hours and talk with your child about the observed effects.

<KeyTakeaways>
- **Oxidation** is a loss of electrons that happens when something reacts with oxygen - rusting iron and a browning apple are both oxidation.
- In a cut apple, enzymes called **phenols** react with oxygen and turn into brown melanin (enzymatic browning).
- **Antioxidants** slow oxidation down by giving up their own electrons first, protecting other molecules.
- **Citric acid** in lemon juice is a natural antioxidant, so the brushed slice stays fresh far longer.
- The same antioxidants (like vitamins C and E) protect our cells from free radicals and are used to preserve food.

## Frequently Asked Questions

<FAQ>
<Question question="Why do apples turn brown when you cut them?">
Cutting an apple breaks its cells and exposes enzymes called phenols to the oxygen in the air. The oxygen reacts with these enzymes and turns them into brown melanin, the very same pigment that colors our skin. This process is called enzymatic browning, or oxidation.
</Question>
<Question question="How does lemon juice stop an apple from turning brown?">
Lemon juice is full of citric acid, which is a natural antioxidant with a low pH. The oxygen reacts with the acid first, before it can reach the apple's enzymes. As long as some acid is left, the enzymes are protected and the apple stays pale.
</Question>
<Question question="What is oxidation in simple terms?">
Oxidation is when an atom or molecule loses electrons, usually while reacting with oxygen. Iron turning to rust and an apple turning brown are both everyday examples. The opposite process, gaining electrons, is called reduction.
</Question>
<Question question="What are antioxidants and why are they important?">
Antioxidants are molecules that slow down oxidation by giving up their own electrons first, protecting other molecules from damage. In our bodies they help keep harmful free radicals in check, and in food they act as preservatives that stop spoilage.
</Question>
<Question question="Besides lemon, what else keeps a cut apple fresh?">
Any acid works: orange, pineapple, or vinegar. You can also slow browning by dropping slices in cold water (less oxygen reaches them), keeping them cold in the fridge, or briefly heating the apple, which deactivates the browning enzymes.
</Question>
<Question question="Is the apple oxidation experiment safe for kids?">
Yes. The only step that needs an adult is slicing the apple with a knife. Everything else  - squeezing the lemon, brushing on the juice, and watching the slices over a few hours is completely safe for children.
</Question>
<Question question="How long does the apple oxidation experiment take?">
Setup takes about ten minutes, and you'll see a clear difference between the two slices within one to two hours. Check again at three hours for the most dramatic comparison.
</Question>
</FAQ>
